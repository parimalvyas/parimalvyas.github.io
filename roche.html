There was Andres and Evan from the Marketing team. Mike from the iPDM team, the B2C side of Accu-Chek. Amy from the consumer marketing team and Josh from the UI development team. The goal was to bring all the ideas on the table and get a buy-in about the extent of change in the current eCommerce ecosystem.


Pain Points

1. Scattered experiences

The current system looks dated and has too many CTAs which are not visually similar. This leads to confusion. Then ther are multiple links to various platforms which futher leads to more confusion. Past user-testing data confirms the same.

2. Account creation and verification

Accu-Chek being a helathcare device provider cannot support guest cheeck-outs therefore they users to create an account. Account creation is followed by account verification before checkout. This hinders and breaks path to the Checkout process.

3. Multiple disjoint platforms

There are a total of three websites present. One with one-time purchase products, second with subscriptions coupled with a surprose wellness product and third one with the newest product subscriptions. These three different platforms have separate branding and each system has its own problems.

4. Checkout process

This process jumps through three different platforms and base URLs which is undesireable. Users aren't sure if they are being directed through a trustworthy website.

Design Approach

As AccuChek.com is ubiquitous on the mobile, tablet, and web, a Responsive Web Design(RWD) approach was needed.
I decided to make a progressive mobile-first design approach as most of the website traffic was via the mobile. Another reason was that it is easier to scale up the content from the mobile design to a desktop interface.

Final Designs

Accessibility

As the intended audience are diabetics. Over time they could develop diabetic retinopathy.

To help them read better there is a high contrast mode and a text size increase option.




Subscriptions Page

From stepped approach to a "one page" approach for the Subscriptions page to avoid decision fatigue for the users.

Solved pain point:
Users can nows see the total steps required and they can easily go back and forth to make changes before checking out the custom build subscription package.


Cart Page

Cart page distinctly illustrates the Subscriptions and the current products being purchased by the user. It also incorporates relevant pricing along with a "Promo Code" section.

Solved pain point:
Visibility of system status in terms of distinguising which cart items are Subscriptions and which are one off purchases with an option to modify and remove each one of them.




Sign In/Register Page

This page has two clear CTAs for Sign-in and Register with a clean and balanced design for better readability. It even has suggested OAuth logins which users were positive about in the User Testing.

Solved pain point:
The need for a verification with the OAuth Facebook and Google Login pages. This was a major drop-off point for the users while checking out.


Shipping Page

The Shipping page adapts as according to the users be it first time users without saved addresses or returning users who can quickly checkout with pre-filled information.

Solved pain point:
Earlier the system did not "remember" addresses and returning customers had to go through the entire process of putting in thier address to checkout.




Billing Page

The Billing page was a seperate page in the old system and as an MVP the team wanted to have a seperate page which could be accessed according to the user choices. This page has a nifty "Same as Shipping Address Button" to quickly choose the same address as before if the users change their mind to give a new address.

Solved pain point:
The page is automatically skipped if the user chooses to set the Billing Address same as the Shipping Address rather than being a mandatory step earlier.


Payment Page

The Payment page still lives on a third party page of Adyen but to bridge the experience: the interactions and look and feel are in sync with the new Checkout system.

Solved pain point:
The page is designed to make the users feel there is little or no transistion to the payement page since we cannot move to a Roche hosted payment page due to legal and techincal challenges.




Confirmation Page

This page gives a summary of the entire order. From the order number to the shipping and billing information to the products ordered.

Solved pain point:
This page has become transperent and information rich. Earlier only the order number was shown as a confirmation.

What I did about it: Made sure the my designs had a staged approch: fulfilled the current needs and created a final desired state for the designs.

Key Takeaways

Listen and observe what the users do

More than listening to what users say, we need to observe what they do while testing a prototype. Over multiple instances uses said they wanted to buy a set of test strips but while choosing options during the tests users did mental calculations on how many test strips they might need and it always turned out to be different than what they had predicted.

Technology imapcts design

It is always best to consider the technology and its limitations to find workarounds and come up with a better solution. Looking at the technical architechture and understanding the amount of time and effort required for changes defines te he best MVP.

Design with stakeholders

Utilize the domain expertise of the people who have been there more than you. I conducted workshops with these experts to understand their perspective on how they envisioned the system. Thier thoughts and ideas helped me shape a better buying experience for the users. Doing this makes them feel involved and thereby being less resistant to changes.

Be flexible and patient

Designing in the enterprise domain especially in healthcare is going to be slow. Accept it. There are multiple approvals and legal clearances thare are needed before things can be changed. Design incementally, have mutiple versions starting form the MVP to the best possible solution.
