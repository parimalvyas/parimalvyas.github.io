Exploring The Problem Space

The Challenge

As social media has grown to become a cornerstone of everyday life in the 21st century, it has provided a major platform for users to not only socially interact with each other, but also keep them connected and up to date with the world around them.

The false news epidemic in social media severely hinders people’s access to authentic information since around 67% of the U.S. adults get news from online media and 57% distrust that information. (PEW Research 2018)

Impact of misinformation or fake news

These inaccurately reported news, otherwise known as “false news”, have become an epidemic in recent times which have had enormous consequences in social, political and economic spheres of society. The incidents of false news centering the 2016 U.S. Presidential Election and the Brexit movement in the U.K. prove how various interest groups can utilize social media platforms for their personal gain. Examples of this include the false news business ran by a Macedonian youth group (Tandoc & Ling, 2018), allegations of the use of Facebook for political gain during the 2016 U.S. election (Allcott & Gentzkow, 2018), and anti-refugee false news and hate crimes in Germany (Muller & Schwarz, 2018), all of which impacted society significantly.

Refined Problem Statement

Identifying and reporting potential viral fake information using social computing conceptual models since existing mechanisms are not effective due to the large volume of data and fast diffusion of false news.

Research Synthesis

Insights from Contextual Enquiry

From our primary research from Twitter users, and our secondary research from scholarly articles on false news, we have identified several key findings of this problem space:

False news is highly reactive to real-world events

The propagation of false news is often fueled by political, religious, racial, and financial motives, and frequently becomes abundant during major events such as elections, social movements, and violent incidents

Individuals on social media regularly encounter false news

It has been very easy for false news to spread unchecked in high quantities, and organizational measures to control it is limited and imperfect. As individuals become more aware of this, they must employ their own strategies to identify and avoid misinformation.

False news has a negative societal impact

The saturation of false news makes people powerless to access authentic information and lead to the discrediting of legitimate news reporting. Various groups disseminate misinformation to influence people’s perception of their views and ideologies which could mislead an entire nation or society.

Designing solutions for false news is an ongoing challenge

Initiatives like Facebook’s flagging options in doubtful contents (Morgan, 2018); changing algorithms (Allcott et al., 2018); detecting bots (Laser et al., 2018); human fact-checkers (Temming, 2018) and Twitter’s rumor debunking algorithms (Liu et al., 2015), all brought a little to no effective solutions to the problem because none of these steps is individually effective.

User Persona

Key User Requirements

In coming up with a design to address this issue while also taking the previous challenges in consideration, we outline three key requirements:

  • Identify false news and display feedback showing the user the degree in which a piece of information is true/accurate or how much bias is in it (like in a spectrum).

  • Implement preventative measures to curb the spread of false news.

  • Acknowledge previously failed implementations for controlling false news, either working around or building upon them.

    • How should we respond?

    Our primary research suggests a human-machine collaborative approach (an infrastructure enabled by both machine-driven and human fact-checking and an automated glossary of fake/hate/extremists terms) will work best to counter this persistent problem. Also due to the vastness of the problem we decided to focus on the portential virality of a post to mitigate impacts.

    Ideation and Solutions

    Storyboarding

    Combining crowdsourced fact-checking, algorithms, and machine learning to identify false news. Once a viral post hits a certain benchmark of flags, the fact-checking systems are enacted. If the article is concluded to be fake, it registers that article as false news which is explicitly stated to users.

    In a conversation between two friends, where one of them brings up a questionable news article. One friend exclaims that the article is fake, and explains to his friend by showing him the truth finder’s evaluation of the article.
    The user sees a news article posted by an account with malicious content and multiple users report it which triggers the fact-checking engine which checks for the credibility if the information and the reliability of the user account.

    Design Decisions

    Using Twitter as a medium as other social media platforms do not have flexible APIs to mine data easily.

    Identify false news and display feedback to the user while also facilitating social computing with crowdsourced participation.

    Implement preventative measures to curb the spread of false news.

    Acknowledge previously ineffective implementations for controlling false news, either working around or building upon them.


    Design Solution

    Human-Machine-Human Collaborated fact-checker engine implemented as a Twitter Client, the TwitterTruth. Crowdsourced & AI fact-checking of posts & linked articles. Re-check critical information using a human fact-checker and display feedback for users.

    • Testing and Evaluation

    Usability Tests

    During our product evaluations, we conducted 4 sessions with users to evaluate two main tasks detailing the main features of our app while doing a think-aloud protocol. These two tasks were:

  • I want to view, flag, and find the truth evaluation of a news post on Twitter.
  • I want to find out more about a user using his profile to find potential bot accounts.

    • For each task, metrics were collected on time-on-task and task success. After each task was completed, two Likert-scale user reflection questions were asked:

  • How easy was this task, on a scale of 1 to 5? (1 being easiest and 5 being hardest)
  • How useful was the app feature for this task, on a scale of 1 to 5? (1 being most useful and 5 being least useful)

    • Recommendations

    From our evaluations, it is clear that the first recommendation is that our app would be in need of stronger ‘help’ and navigation features so that first-time users can more easily learn how to use the app, what functions it is meant to do, and how it differs from standard Twitter.

    More recommendations from participants were suggested in making the app more rich in features.

    One key recommendation which we got from our presentation feedback was the need for expert human fact-checkers in the evaluation process. This is because the crowdsourced flagging and machine AI evaluation may not be enough to give the user a substantial evaluation on whether or not a news post is truthful.
