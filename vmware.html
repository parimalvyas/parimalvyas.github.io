Challenges Faced

Balancing Product and Engineering

In my role, I frequently interacted with the product and engineering teams with preliminary designs and wireframes in order to narrow down the scope of the project. I quickly saw that too many voices in the room caused misalignment between the agenda of the meeting and the outcome. To define the user-flow, I had smaller syncs with the product manager and a staff engineer individually before jumping to a meeting with multiple stakeholders.

Follow up shamelessly

One of the biggest oversights I did was to wait on the product manager to get back to me about the next steps. This led to me being stuck in limbo multiple times in one of the quarters. After learning this the hard way, I decided to enforce our 1x1 weekly meetings and make sure I proactively track progress on the next steps.

Key Takeaways

Engineering v/s Product

The engineering team had inputs that shaped a feature implementation roadmap, since VCD is a highly technical product. Working with Product team we defined the MVP (minimum viable product) but working with the engineering team we defined the MEP (minimum enjoyable product)

Designing for the flow

Engineering and highly technical users are looking for a balance in the complexity of their tasks. If it is too easy/automated they feel they have little or no control but if they have to jump between multiple documentations and API definitions it becomes trial and error which leads to frustration.

Enterprise users want their time back

Enterprise users want to be engaged while doing a task but the time to value needs to be shortened. Time is valuable and workflows should save them time while providing value.

Evangelize design

This may sound redundant that as a designer I had seen the need to evangelize design to various groups from Engineering, to the PMs and the leadership team to gain visibility, show value and to onboard them in investing time in design activities. Follow-up with stakeholders even if it is uncomforable.
